Adventures in cooking: Chilli-Lime Salmon

Yesterday was Show Day, a kind of holiday in lieu that we in Canterbury are awarded because our actual provincial anniversary (16 December) is deemed too close to Christmas for a holiday weekend, so we get a Friday off in the middle of November instead. Traditionally, weather on Show Day is gorgeous, and yesterday certainly held up its end of the bargain, with a balmy (for Christchurch) 26°C (79°F). After a day in the sun, I felt like cooking my favourite dish, and, what the hell, sharing it with the Internet.

Like pretty much anything I cook, it’s fast, rich, and carries very little regard for dietary balance. To my credit, though, it includes both fish and fresh fruit, so it’s probably better than most.

Step 1: Go to the shop

The first thing you need is a nice bit of salmon. I use a salmon steak, which is a nice thick slab of meat backed by silvery skin; salmon happened to be on special at the supermarket yesterday, so even the smallest piece I could find was pretty massive, and cost less than five dollars. While you’re at the supermarket, pick up a couple of fresh limes: everything else you need will probably be in your cupboards at home.

Step 2: Prepare

Take a sharp knife and score the salmon, working across the grain of the meat at about half-inch intervals, and taking the cuts down about three quarters of the way through the steak (don’t slice right through). Into each cut, drizzle a little sweet chilli sauce. Don’t overdo the chilli, or it’ll be all you taste, but you want enough to coat the inside of the cuts. Then, finally, cut your limes into very thin wedges and place them, flesh-down, in the cuts.

Step 2A: Entirely optional

You can totally skip this part, but I enjoy drizzling the whole assembly with tequila. It gives it a bit of tang, and at the same time I find it mellows out the chilli sauce.

Step 3: Grill

How you do this depends on your preferred grilling method. I use a George Foreman grill, because I have one, but I imagine it would work just as well wrapped in aluminium foil and placed in the oven. The George Foreman does have the advantage that you can lift up the top and see how it’s going from time to time, which is important for me because I can’t really cook.

Having said that, here is my theory on cooking this dish: as the limes heat up, they release their juice into the fish, which keeps the meat moist, making it basically impossible to screw it up. The main thing is that the chilli sauce is mostly sugar, so if it gets too hot, it will caramelise, and then burn, and that’s hard to get off the grill. I’d say no more than five or six minutes, and you’re done.

Step 4: Serve

While you’re doing this, of course, you’ve also cooked some rice and made a green salad: maybe some baby spinach, lettuce, and capsicum, which is what you can see in the picture. That is an awful lot of salmon, but since my partner is vegetarian, I’m obliged to eat the whole thing. Life is cruel that way.

Step 5: Enjoy (slowly)

I say “slowly”, not because you’ll regret having finished it (although that is certainly true), but because this particular cut of salmon is full of tiny, translucent bones that really hurt if you try to swallow them. Please enjoy responsibly.

So, that’s it. Ridiculously simple, delicious, and not entirely unhealthy salmon meal, in six minutes, for six dollars. Bon appetit!

What rolls down stairs, alone or in pairs?

I lose count of the number of times I enter passwords every day. Sure, there are a few brave souls trying to fix that, but it’s a way off yet. I still think, though, that for all its ubiquity, login is broken.

I’m not necessarily talking about interfaces here (although I’ve seen some truly egregious specimens): I mean the word “login”. Log in is an example of a phrasal verb:

  • I log in
  • you log in
  • he/she/it logs in
  • we log in
  • you log in
  • they log in

Now, if you accept the convention that button text should be a verb phrase in the imperative form (“connect to server”, “launch torpedoes”, etc), you want the second person singular form: log in. Not login. You don’t login to a website any more than you goin to town, eatup your meal or shootdown spurious grammatical examples.

If you think about it, login is clearly not a verb:

  • I logged in
  • I logginned (?)
  • I logan (?)
  • I logun (?)

So where does “login” come from? Well, check this out:

  • He is so hung up on this phrasal verb thing.
  • That’s just one of his many grammatical hang-ups (or hangups, if you prefer).
  • I looked up her number.
  • The lookup algorithm on my phone is hideously inefficient.

See the pattern? Phrasal verbs get turned into nouns or adjectives, and the space between the verb and the preposition gets sublimated into a hyphen, or evaporates altogether. So, you can talk about “the login screen”, or “automatic login”, but not “please login to get started”.

A similar thing is happening with “every day” and “everyday”:

  • This shirt is great for everyday wear (adjective)
  • I wear this shirt every day (adverbial phrase)
  • I fail at grammar everyday (wrong)

…and there’s a worrying tendency to use “into” wherever the words “in” and “to” are adjacent, even if it makes no sense:

I finally gave into her demands.

Hey, wow! It’s another phrasal verb! Since in is part of the verb to give in, you can’t squish it up with to. Really, you should only use into when you’re crawling up inside something like a warm kitten. Which is not as often as you’d think.

It’s one of those weird grammatical tweaks that requires a bit of knowledge to get right, but I think that’s why it gets on my nerves so much. Still, a little bit of consistency would be nice.
